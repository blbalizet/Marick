Reading — Routers

Routers

You can't go to the store and buy the internet like you can buy a computer. But you can buy a router — and that's close enough.

Routers make it possible for devices to connect to the internet and share data. If you've ever connected to WiFi at home, at school, or at a coffee shop, you were using a router. A router passes information between one or more computer networks. It acts as a gateway — sitting between the internet connection coming in through a modem, and the local network that your devices use to talk to each other.

When a unit of data — called a packet — arrives at a router, the router identifies where that packet is trying to go and calculates the best way to get it there. Think of it like GPS. The router compares the destination against a routing table — basically a set of rules for forwarding data, which is itself an algorithm — and picks the best path through the network.

The router also keeps track of which internet traffic goes to which device. That's what prevents your emails and web searches from showing up on someone else's screen instead of your own.

Routers can be physical standalone devices, or virtual — meaning the router is a software instance running on a machine rather than a box you can hold.

The Connection

A router is hardware. It has a job: move data from place to place.

But how does a piece of hardware know what to do the moment it's turned on — before any software is running, before any human touches it?

That's where firmware comes in.

Reading — Firmware

Firmware

Firmware is a type of software that's embedded into hardware devices during manufacturing to control their functionality. Firmware runs in the background and controls the hardware. It's not meant to interact with human users directly — it's the thing that tells the machine how to be a machine.

Unlike the apps or programs you install on a device, firmware is permanently stored in non-volatile memory — meaning it doesn't disappear when the power is cut. It provides instructions for how the device is supposed to work, minimizes hardware-level security vulnerabilities, and acts as a bridge between the hardware and the operating system.

Firmware is the first thing that runs when a device powers on. It sends instructions to the processor before anything else happens. In simple devices — like a remote control — firmware runs continuously because there's no software to take over. In more complex devices like computers, multiple pieces of firmware communicate with each other to accomplish a shared goal, like loading the OS.

Here's a concrete example: a computer without firmware wouldn't know how to detect its own hard drive. A hard drive without embedded firmware wouldn't know how fast to spin — or when to stop. A traffic light uses firmware to know when to change colors. Your phone, your printer, your router, your TV — all of them have firmware running underneath everything else you see.

In personal computers, the most common examples of firmware are BIOS and UEFI — the programs that run the moment you press the power button, before Windows or any other OS loads.

Reading — Firmware (cont.)

Types, Updates, and One Warning

There are three types of firmware. Low-level firmware is permanently stored in non-volatile chips — it can't be modified or updated, ever. High-level firmware is stored in flash memory, so updates are possible and manufacturers do push them. Subsystem firmware is part of an embedded system that can operate somewhat independently — like a server's power system, which runs almost separately from the rest of the machine.

Device manufacturers regularly release firmware updates to fix bugs, keep devices secure, and maintain compatibility. Your phone gets these. Game consoles get them. TVs get them — usually as a prompt asking you to download and install.

One last thing worth knowing: you normally can't delete firmware. If you somehow did, your device would become what's called a brick — powered on but completely non-functional. It would be like wiping out the device's brain.

The short version:

The router is hardware doing a job. Firmware is what tells it how. Without firmware, it's just a box.

Reading — Squeaky Clean

Squeaky Clean

Keeping a computer clean can increase the longevity of the unit for a significant amount of time. Additionally, as dust and dirt build up in the unit, it can become a fire hazard or cause significant damage to PC components.

Along with overheating, dust buildup can cost you performance. As temperatures rise, your CPU and GPU will begin to drop below their maximum boost and turbo clocks. In severe cases it can lead to significant throttling, if not shutdowns when your system is under a heavy load.

The most important thing to consider when cleaning your case is that we want to make it possible for cool, fresh air to get into the case and hot air to get out. If dust filters are clogged or there's excess dust collected around the heat sinks, that's not going to be possible and your system could overheat.

To counter that, you want to do everything you can to eliminate dust and leave your system fresh and clean.

Note: Whenever cleaning your PC or its components, you need to take extra special care about static damage. Using an anti-static wristband is ideal, but if you don't have one to hand, make sure to regularly ground yourself by touching your case or PSU housing.
