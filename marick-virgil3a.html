Character Archetypes

Now — a lesson from Unit 2.

We're going to go through some character archetypes. These are the types of characters that show up in games, movies, and stories over and over.

Same deal as the vocab: I'll show you the definition, and then ask you to type the character type. Short, easy, repetitive — and yes, that's by design.

Archetype 1 of 11
The Hero is usually the character played by the player. They embark on a transformative journey — facing and overcoming challenges. Heroes are selfless; they make sacrifices to protect the people they care about. You'll find this character in action, adventure, and role-playing games. Think Gordon Freeman from Half-Life.
Archetype 2 of 11
The Shadow is the evil character — the opposite of the hero. Like Agent Smith in The Matrix. To increase tension, the shadow's identity may not be revealed until the end. Sometimes the shadow and the hero are the same person — the shadow being the hero's dark side. Think the Dark Prince in Prince of Persia: The Two Thrones.
Archetype 3 of 11
🔁 Yeah, I know — this probably feels a little repetitive. That's the point. It's called spaced repetition and it genuinely works. Your brain is building a filing system right now.
The Mentor acts as the hero's advisor. They guide the hero's quest by providing hints and tips for overcoming challenges. Deckard Cain from the Diablo series is a classic mentor. You'll find this character in action, action-adventure, and role-playing games.
Archetype 4 of 11
An Ally helps the hero on their quest — usually as a sidekick assisting with tasks that are difficult for the hero to do alone. In Fallout 3, Dogmeat the dog is one of several allies you can recruit. This character type typically shows up in action-adventure and role-playing games.
Archetype 5 of 11
The Guardian blocks the hero's progress until the hero proves they are worthy of facing what lies ahead. In The Matrix Reloaded, the Oracle's guardian Seraph stops Neo until he proves himself in battle. Sometimes the guardian isn't even a person — it can be fear or self-doubt.
Archetype 6 of 11
🔁 Still going. Still by design. You're almost halfway through the archetypes.
The Trickster is a neutral character who likes to play pranks on the hero. Their antics may slow the hero down or force an alternate path — but they're usually harmless and provide comic relief. Peeves the Poltergeist from the Harry Potter games is a classic example.
Archetype 7 of 11
A Herald triggers a change in the storyline. In Assassin's Creed 2, the hero lives a happy life until his father and brothers are killed — that tragic event changes everything. The slain family members serve as heralds. This character type is common in role-playing games.
Archetype 8 of 11
The Protagonist is similar to the hero — the game is played from their perspective. They're usually depicted as strong and likeable, but they can have weaknesses too. Those weaknesses actually make them more human and easier for players to connect with.
Archetype 9 of 11
🔁 Three more after this. You're almost there. And yes — still doing this on purpose.
Co-protagonists are characters who team up with the protagonist to fight the antagonist. They share the spotlight and work alongside the main character rather than just supporting from the sidelines.
Archetype 10 of 11
Supporting Characters aren't the main focus of the story, but they're essential — they help maintain the narrative or keep the hero focused on what matters. Think of them as the background crew that makes the whole story work.
Archetype 11 of 11
The Antagonist is the game's villain — but unlike the classical Shadow, the antagonist may not be purely evil. They might just have a conflicting personality or a different point of view than the protagonist. The conflict is real; the motivation isn't always simple.
